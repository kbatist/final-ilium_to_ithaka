Poem Three
"The Sirens' Call"

"Come closer, Odysseus—" the sirens sing, The sea remembers every voice, each song a thread in the history of man. Odysseus listens, bound by ropes, his yearning pressed against the tide. The Sirens sing not of death, but of legacy— the fragile weight of choices, the echo of desire.

Their call is a mirror, reflecting the hunger of centuries: heroes who sought glory, lovers who sought home, wanderers who sought meaning. It is the same refrain, a chorus of longing, woven into the fabric of time.

et history is not kind. The song lingers, a reminder that man’s story is written in yearning, in the ache of what cannot be held. Trumpets blairing, you walked out, without a backward glance.

I saved you a seat and then you want to stand. The Sirens’ call is eternal, a melody of human frailty, a hymn to the endless search for belonging.
