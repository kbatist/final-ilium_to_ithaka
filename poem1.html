Poem One
"Achilles' Lament"

"Achilles, come down—" the gods whisper through the smoke of Troy, their voices trembling like strings of a lyre. “See the world as it is, not as it should be,” yet his heart is bound to Patroclus, a tether stronger than bronze, fragile as breath.

He kneels in the dust, the war drums fading into silence, and grief becomes his armor. “Don’t listen to what you’ve been told,” the chorus pleads, but he cannot unhear the shattering— the fall of a friend, the collapse of a future.

"The river carries his sorrow," each step heavy with the weight of fate. “Achilles, Achilles, just put down the stone,” but rage is the only language left, and heartbreak the only crown. He is both hero and ruin, a boy who loved too fiercely, a man who could not be saved.
